# DoseGuard Citations

Use these sources distinctly. Do not collapse them into one citation or imply WHO endorsed the full product logic chain.

## 1. Cause framing only

- World Health Organization. "Antimicrobial resistance." Fact sheet, 21 November 2023.
  Link: https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/antimicrobial-resistance
  Use for: "Antimicrobial resistance is a major global health threat" and "WHO identifies misuse and overuse of antimicrobials as major drivers."
  Note: This source supports the AMR problem framing. It should not be used to imply WHO identified the dispensing handoff as the specific intervention point.

## 2. Product-evidence bridge: prescription instructions are often misunderstood

- Wolf MS, Davis TC, Shrank W, et al. "To err is human: patient misinterpretations of prescription drug label instructions." Patient Education and Counseling. 2007;67(3):293-300.
  Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17587533/
  Why it fits: The abstract reports that misunderstanding of dosage instructions on prescription drug labels is common, and that patients with low literacy had higher rates of misunderstanding.
  Safe use: Cite this when linking DoseGuard to the real problem of instruction misunderstanding, especially in low-literacy populations.

## 3. Design choice support: pictogram-based materials can improve comprehension

- Ng AWY, Chan AHS, Ho VWS. "Comprehension by older people of medication information with or without supplementary pharmaceutical pictograms." Applied Ergonomics. 2017;58:167-175.
  Link: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0003687016301168
  Why it fits: The abstract highlights that adding supplementary pharmaceutical pictograms improved understanding of medication information.
  Safe use: Cite this to support the design choice of a language-light, pictogram-supported checklist. Do not overclaim that pictograms alone ensure correct use in every setting.

## Optional backup source for pictogram support

- Yin HS, Parker RM, Sanders LM, et al. "Pictograms, Units and Dosing Tools, and Parent Medication Errors: A Randomized Study." Pediatrics. 2017;140(1):e20163237.
  Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28759396/
  Why it helps: The abstract describes a randomized study examining whether pictographic diagrams could reduce medication dosing errors.

## Copy guardrails

- WHO supports the cause framing only.
- Wolf et al. supports the label misunderstanding bridge.
- Ng et al. supports the pictogram design choice.
- Avoid saying DoseGuard reduces AMR directly.
- Preferred line: "DoseGuard does not solve antimicrobial resistance. It addresses one practical misuse pathway at the dispensing counter."
